# The Baltic Setting

Battlegroup Clash: Baltics (BC:B) is set in **Estonia** — a NATO member state on the eastern flank of the alliance, sharing a land border with Russia and a maritime border with Finland. Understanding why Estonia is a plausible flashpoint, and what makes it militarily distinctive, is essential context for every scenario in this game.

---

## Strategic Position: Why the Baltics Matter

The three Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania — occupy a narrow strip of the eastern Baltic coastline. Combined, they have a total land area smaller than Germany and a collective population of under six million. Yet their geographic position makes them one of NATO's most consequential — and most exposed — frontiers.

**Key facts:**

- Estonia borders Russia directly to the east (the Narva River forms the border; the city of Narva sits on the Estonian side, Ivangorod on the Russian side)
- Latvia and Lithuania lie to the south, connected to the rest of NATO's land mass only through Poland and the Suwalki corridor
- The Baltic Sea connects to NATO's northern flank via Finland (NATO member since 2023) and Sweden (NATO member since 2024)
- All three states joined NATO in 2004; Article 5 collective defence obligations fully apply

NATO's central problem in the Baltics is **strategic depth, or the absence of it**. From the Russian border to the sea, Estonia is approximately 200 km at its widest. A fast-moving Russian armoured column could, in a worst-case scenario, reach Tallinn within hours. NATO planners have accordingly treated the Baltic states as a tripwire problem since at least 2014.

---

## NATO Enhanced Forward Presence (eFP)

Following Russia's annexation of Crimea and the invasion of eastern Ukraine in 2014, NATO convened the Warsaw Summit in 2016 and agreed to deploy four multinational **Enhanced Forward Presence (eFP)** battlegroups — one in each Baltic state and one in Poland. Each battlegroup is led by a **framework nation** and augmented by forces from multiple NATO allies.

### eFP Estonia

The Estonia battlegroup is led by the **United Kingdom**. The UK contribution is drawn from various armoured infantry and armoured cavalry regiments; the battlegroup is based at Tapa, Estonia, approximately 100 km east of Tallinn. Contributing nations have included France, Denmark, Belgium, Iceland, and others, rotating in and out.

**Key characteristics of eFP Estonia:**

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Framework nation | United Kingdom |
| Main base | Tapa Army Base, Lääne-Viru County |
| Composition | ~1,000–1,500 personnel (varies by rotation) |
| Core capability | Challenger 2 MBTs (UK), Warrior IFVs, wheeled recce, artillery |
| Mission | Credible deterrent presence; trigger for Article 5 response |

The battlegroup is explicitly **not sized to defeat a Russian army-level advance unaided**. Its purpose is deterrence: ensuring that any Russian military action against Estonia cannot be conducted without killing British (and other NATO) soldiers, thereby guaranteeing a full Article 5 response. In NATO parlance, this is called a "tripwire" force — though NATO planners increasingly prefer the term **"in-place deterrent."**

BC:B represents this force directly. The [[nato|NATO]] faction is centred on a generic European [[nato|NATO]] **Battlegroup** (approximately battalion-sized), explicitly modelled on British-led eFP structure.

### eFP Latvia

Framework nation: **Canada**. Based at Camp Ādaži, north of Rīga. Contributing nations include Albania, Czech Republic, Iceland, Italy, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Slovakia, Slovenia, and Spain.

### eFP Lithuania

Framework nation: **Germany**. Based at Rukla. Contributing nations include Belgium, Croatia, Czech Republic, France, Luxembourg, Netherlands, Norway, and others.

### eFP Poland

Framework nation: **United States**. Based at Orzysz (Bemowo Piskie). The US presence in Poland is substantially larger than a single eFP battlegroup; the 82nd Airborne Division, V Corps, and other formations rotate through Poland on a near-continuous basis.

## Post-2022: The Shift from Forward Presence to Forward Defence

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 was a watershed for Baltic NATO posture. The eFP concept — a deterrent tripwire of approximately 1,000 soldiers per battlegroup — was widely recognised as militarily insufficient to halt a conventional Russian thrust. NATO's Madrid Summit (June 2022) agreed a significant posture shift:

- The four eFP battlegroups would be **scaled up from battlegroup to brigade level** over time
- Each brigade (~3,000–5,000 soldiers) would be capable of meaningful resistance, not just tripwire deterrence
- National reinforcement plans were accelerated: the UK committed to scaling eFP Estonia toward a full brigade; Germany made similar commitments in Lithuania
- NATO's Allied Rapid Reaction Corps (ARRC) revived heavy combined-arms exercises at scale in the Baltic region
- Finland's NATO accession (April 2023) transformed the strategic picture: Finland's 280,000-strong reserve force and shared border with Russia added substantial depth to NATO's northern flank and eliminated the sea gap that previously isolated the Baltics from Scandinavia

As of 2024–2025, the eFP in Estonia retains its UK framework with Challenger 2 tanks and a growing footprint at Tapa, though the full brigade-level force exists primarily on paper and in reinforcement planning rather than as a permanently forward-deployed formation.

**BC:B sits in this transition window.** The scenario posture — a single battlegroup, below-strength, with limited supplies — reflects the actual eFP force as it existed from 2017 onward, before brigade-scale reinforcement could arrive.

## The Suwalki Gap

No discussion of Baltic defence is complete without the **Suwalki Gap** (also written "Suwałki Gap"). This is the single most strategically important piece of real estate in European military planning.

The Suwalki Gap is a **~104 km land corridor** on the Polish-Lithuanian border, running between:
- **Kaliningrad Oblast** (Russian exclave) to the west
- **Belarus** to the east

It is the **only land connection between the Baltic states and the rest of NATO**. If Russian and Belarusian forces were to seize this corridor simultaneously — Kaliningrad forces moving east, Belarusian forces moving west — NATO's Baltic members would be **physically cut off** from reinforcement by land.

```
[NATO territory: Poland]  ←— Suwalki Gap (~104 km) —→  [Belarus]
                                       ↑
                               Kaliningrad (Russia)
```

The implications for BC:B scenarios are direct:

1. **No reinforcement corridor**: a scenario set after a Russian seizure of the Suwalki Gap would leave the eFP battlegroup fighting without the prospect of ground reinforcement. Resupply would be by air or sea only — hence the game's **Scarcity** mechanic and limited ammunition.
2. **Strategic isolation**: the forces on the map are the forces available. There is no reserve waiting to be committed.
3. **Time pressure**: NATO planners have estimated the eFP battlegroup could hold for 60–72 hours before being overwhelmed by a concerted Russian advance, absent significant air support. This maps directly to the game's short scenario duration (5–6 turns; ~1.5–3 hours of game-time).

The Suwalki Gap is not merely a theoretical concern. NATO exercises (e.g., Iron Wolf, Defender series) routinely rehearse its defence. The gap's narrowness means a relatively small defending force can contest it — but the political and logistical difficulty of pre-positioning enough force to hold it credibly has been a persistent alliance challenge.

## Russian Military Posture: West and Northwest

### Western Military District

Russia's military forces relevant to Estonia and the Baltic states fall primarily under the **Western Military District (WMD)**, headquartered in Saint Petersburg. The WMD covers the approaches to Estonia, Latvia, and the Gulf of Finland.

Key formations near the Estonian border include:

| Formation | Location | Capability |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| 6th Combined Arms Army | Saint Petersburg region | Motorised Rifle, Armour |
| 1st Guards Tank Army (WMD) | Moscow-Smolensk region | Heavy armour; rapid reinforcement |
| Baltic Fleet | Baltiysk, Kaliningrad; Baltiysk-Pilau | Amphibious landing, sea denial, missiles |
| Pskov 76th Guards Air Assault Division | Pskov (~30 km from Estonian border) | Airborne / air assault; rapid insertion |

The **76th Guards Air Assault Division** at Pskov is particularly significant for Estonia scenarios. Pskov is approximately 30 km from the Estonian border. An airborne assault by elements of the 76th — seized airstrips, helicopter-inserted blocking forces — could theoretically precede or accompany a conventional ground advance, creating chaos behind eFP lines before ground forces could consolidate.

### Kaliningrad Oblast

Kaliningrad is a Russian exclave on the Baltic Sea, flanked by Poland to the south and Lithuania to the north and east. It is a hardened military bastion:

- **Baltic Fleet headquarters** at Baltiysk
- Significant ground forces: 11th Guards Army (motorised rifle and armoured formations)
- **S-400 surface-to-air missile (SAM) systems** providing air defence coverage over much of the eastern Baltic
- **Iskander-M short-range ballistic missile (SRBM) systems** capable of reaching targets across the Baltics and into Poland — range approximately 500 km
- **Bastion coastal defence missile systems** providing sea-denial coverage over the eastern Baltic

Kaliningrad's missile arsenal creates a **contested airspace** environment over the Baltic region without Russia launching a single fixed-wing aircraft. This is directly reflected in BC:B: neither side has available fixed-wing aviation or attack helicopters — the airspace above the battlefield is too dangerous for either side to operate freely.

### Pre-War Attrition

BC:B also assumes that Russian forces are engaged in an ongoing conflict — and that **attrition has been significant**. This is reflected in the [[russia|Russian]] faction's lower [[force-elements|Troop Quality (TQ)]] and [[force-elements|Platoon (PL)]] Resilience of 3 (versus NATO's 4). Russian units committed to a Baltic operation would not be fresh — they would represent forces that had already been degraded by operations elsewhere (implicitly: in Ukraine), refilled with mobilised reservists, and committed to a new theatre under severe logistics strain.

## Estonian Terrain: Military Geography

Estonia is not the flat, featureless plain that many assume. It is a country of subtle but militarily significant terrain that strongly favours defence and significantly constrains armoured manoeuvre.

### Forests

Approximately **50% of Estonia's land area is forested**. These are not ornamental woodlands — they are dense boreal and mixed forests of spruce, pine, birch, and alder, with thick undergrowth. Key military implications:

- Tracked and wheeled vehicles are largely confined to roads and tracks through forested areas
- Infantry can move and conceal effectively in woodland, but loses its organic firepower advantages
- Dense forest creates excellent **Line of Sight (LoS)** interruption — direct-fire engagement ranges can collapse to under 300m in thick canopy
- Forest also limits UAS surveillance — drones struggle to track targets beneath a closed canopy
- Conversely, forest *edges* (the treeline) are major tactical features: defended treelines are extremely difficult to approach over open ground

The BC:B map represents this with **Light Wood** (partial obstruction, passable) and **Dense Wood** (full obstruction, No-Go for vehicles except on roads) terrain categories. See [[terrain]] for full effects.

### Wetlands and Bogs

Estonia has extensive **peat bogs (Estonians: "raba") and wetlands**, particularly in western and central Estonia. Many of these are trafficable on foot in summer but become marsh in spring thaw or after rainfall. In winter they freeze solid and can support vehicle traffic. Off-road armoured movement in Estonia is therefore highly season-dependent.

Military implications:
- Bogs and wetlands channel armoured axes to roads and elevated ground
- Historical example: Soviet partisans and Estonian Forest Brothers used boggy terrain to evade mechanised sweeps during and after WW2
- Modern context: armoured thrust options are fewer than the map suggests — Russian planners would need to identify and maintain secure road axes, making those roads predictable and targetable

### Rivers

Estonia has several significant river systems. The most militarily important:

| River | Significance |
|-------|-------------|
| **Narva** | Forms the entire eastern border with Russia; major tank obstacle; urban crossing at Narva-Ivangorod |
| **Emajõgi** | Connects Lake Võrtsjärv and Lake Peipus through Tartu (Estonia's second city); significant obstacle |
| **Pärnu** | Southwestern Estonia; limits approach routes from south toward Tallinn |
| **Lake Peipus / Peipsi** | Enormous lake forming ~45% of the eastern border; effectively impassable without bridging |

The Narva River–Lake Peipus line forms a natural defensive barrier along much of Estonia's eastern border. However, the **Narva crossing** is a critical chokepoint: a major road bridge and rail bridge cross here, and the city of Narva (population ~60,000, majority Russian-speaking) sits directly on the border. Seizure of the Narva bridges by Russian forces in a coup de main operation is a scenario that NATO planners have war-gamed extensively.

### Urban Areas

Estonian towns are generally small-to-medium in size, but several are militarily significant:

| Location | Pop. (approx.) | Significance |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Tallinn** | ~450,000 | Capital; port; political centre of gravity |
| **Tartu** | ~95,000 | Emajõgi crossings; southern logistics hub |
| **Narva** | ~60,000 | Border city; majority Russian-speaking; bridge crossing |
| **Tapa** | ~5,500 | eFP base; rail junction; central Estonia logistics node |
| **Jõhvi / Kohtla-Järve** | ~40,000 combined | Industrial northeast; historically significant Russian-speaking population |

Urban areas in Estonia are characterised by **Soviet-era concrete apartment blocks** interspersed with older low-rise construction and surrounded by light industrial zones — urban terrain that provides cover and obstruction in equal measure, channels vehicle movement, and heavily favours the defender.

BC:B maps model these environments with **Light Urban** (partial obstruction) and **Urban** (full obstruction) terrain. See [[terrain]].

### Open Ground and Farmland

Between the forests, open agricultural land provides the long-range fields of fire that armoured and anti-armour weapons require. Estonian farmland tends to occur in river valleys and coastal lowlands. These open areas are both the most mechanised-force-friendly terrain and the most exposed to UAS observation — there is no concealment from drone surveillance in open fields.

This directly underpins BC:B's **contested surveillance** environment: on open terrain, neither side can hide. [[support-assets|Unmanned Aerial Support drone (UAS)]] cover the open ground; the forests offer concealment but restrict movement. The player must constantly trade cover against mobility.

## The Drone-Saturated Battlefield

Ukraine (2022–present) demonstrated what military planners had theorised but not experienced at scale: **cheap commercial drones and First Person View (FPV) munitions** can provide near-total surveillance of an operational area and deliver lethal effect at a fraction of traditional artillery cost.

In the Baltic scenario:

- Both sides operate extensive UAS surveillance grids, covering roads and open terrain in near-real-time
- [[support-assets|First Person View drone (FPV)]] munitions (adapted commercial quadcopters or fixed-wing drones carrying shaped charges or fragmentation warheads) provide on-call precision strike against individual vehicles or infantry positions
- Electronic emissions — radio traffic, radar, artillery — are immediately detected, located, and targeted. This is the [[electronic-warfare|Electronic Warfare (EW)]] / [[electronic-warfare|Electronic Transmission (ETX)]] system in BC:B: every radio call is a targeting cue.
- The result is a battlefield with **no concealment** from surveillance (in open terrain) but **no air superiority** for either side. Both sides are simultaneously all-seeing and all-killable.

This environment makes the **treeline, villages, and riverbanks** the defining terrain features of every engagement — the only places a force can exist without being under constant drone observation.

## Scenario Assumptions: Why These Specific Rules

The game's mechanics are not arbitrary — they map directly to real-world conditions:

| Game Mechanic | Real-World Basis |
|---------------|-----------------|
| No fixed-wing or helicopter aviation | Kaliningrad SAM umbrella; Russian A2/AD (Anti-Access/Area Denial) covering eastern Baltic; contested airspace |
| UAS on every map | Ukraine-derived lesson: small drones provide total low-altitude surveillance at operational scale |
| FPV strikes as off-map support | Commercial and military FPV munitions proven in Ukraine as vehicle-killers at low cost |
| ETX generation from radio use | Russian EW capability (Krasukha, Borisoglebsk-2 systems) can geolocate transmitters in seconds |
| Russian Red Doctrine rigidity | Pre-war Russian doctrine: centralized, plan-driven, with limited authority at company and platoon level |
| Russian lower TQ and Resilience | Post-mobilization Russian units in 2022–present: degraded by losses, filled by mobilized reservists |
| Ammunition scarcity | Both sides in Ukraine experienced critical ammunition shortfalls within weeks; Baltic scenario logistically severed from NATO by Suwalki closure |
| Short scenario duration (5–6 turns) | NATO estimates 60–72 hr window before Baltic eFP is overwhelmed without reinforcement |
| European-centric force (not US) | eFP is a European-led force; US reinforcement contingency involves transit through Suwalki Gap or sea/air lift — not available if gap is seized |

## The Maps

The four BC:B maps (A through D) are drawn from **actual Estonian satellite imagery** by cartographer Shayne Logan. Each map covers a 10km × 10km area at 1:10,000 scale (1cm = 100m). They are composites of real Estonian terrain, not stylised or abstracted geography.

Terrain features players will encounter across the maps:

- **Forested ground** dominating most of the map area, with open patches around farmsteads, river valleys, and roads
- **Road networks**: a few Major Roads (main axes) and numerous Minor Roads (tracks and rural routes)
- **River obstacles** with limited bridge crossing points — each bridge is a tactical objective
- **Small settlement clusters** offering cover and Urban terrain benefits
- **Open fields** at forest edges and along valleys — the ground where armour fights

There are no mountains, no significant urban complexes, and no large open plains. Estonia fights small and close — the range of most engagements on these maps is 500m to 2,000m, determined by the spacing of forest edges and road bends.

## Summary: Why This Scenario is Militarily Interesting

The Baltic scenario presents a tactical and operational problem with no good solutions for either side:

**For NATO:** A small, quality force defending against a numerically superior attacker, in terrain that favours defence but not manoeuvre, with no prospect of air support, limited ammunition, and no ground reinforcement coming. Every [[force-elements|Force Element (FE)]] matters; trading space for time is an option but space is limited.

**For Russia:** A force with doctrinal rigidity, attritional losses, and logistical strain, attacking into dense terrain against a prepared defender with capable anti-armour weapons. Numbers matter but cannot simply be mass-deployed — the roads channel everything into killzones. Artillery and [[support-assets|FPV]] are the equalizer, but ammunition is finite.

The result is a scenario that is simultaneously plausible, historically grounded, and tactically demanding for both players — which is precisely why Estonia was chosen.
